01 — The Problem

THE MACHINE IS IDLE.
THE IDEA IS GONE.

Running three fabrication machines simultaneously means there's always a gap between inspiration and execution. An idea for a new jig hits while you're mid-job on the laser. A customer asks if you could cut something and the conversation moves on. You see a cool design online and think "I should make that" — then forget about it entirely by the time you sit down at the Mac.

The problem isn't motivation or skill. It's that there's no reliable system between "I want to make that" and "it's in the queue." The Bambu sits idle during prime build hours because the ideas that would fill it live in your head, not in a machine-readable list.

And beyond just capturing what to make, there's a second problem: you might spend 3 hours modeling something that already exists as a free download on Thangs. Or pays better to buy from a supplier. Or requires a material you don't stock. Without research, the queue is just a wish list.

QUEUE was built to solve both problems at once: capture the idea with zero friction, then do the research automatically so by the time you're ready to run a job, you know exactly what you're printing, where the file came from, and whether it's worth making at all.
02 — What QUEUE Is

OWL FOR YOUR
MACHINES

QUEUE is a direct descendant of OWL — it's the same architecture, the same Telegram-first interface, the same cron-driven digest loop. The difference is what it does with what you send it.

Where OWL categorizes your general thoughts into pellets and routes them to the right business agent, QUEUE is purpose-built for one thing: managing what you want to fabricate. It receives your build ideas, tags them to the right machine, researches whether a file or design already exists online, and delivers a daily digest showing you what's ready to run and what it found.

The part that makes QUEUE more than just a glorified notes app is what happens after you submit a job. Every entry automatically triggers a background research sweep across the major fabrication file platforms — looking for existing models, SVG packs, design resources, and community builds that match what you described.

The research isn't just a link dump — QUEUE evaluates what it finds. If there are 40 Thingiverse results for "cable management clip" it picks the highest-rated ones, notes the print settings community members recommend, and flags if any require post-processing or supports. If it finds nothing, it says so clearly and marks the job as needs-custom-design.

05 — The Daily Digest

WHAT'S READY TO
RUN THIS MORNING

At 9:15 AM every morning, QUEUE sends a digest — the same format OWL uses, the same pipeline. It compiles everything you've added since the last digest that has a research result, organizes it by machine, and delivers it via Telegram and email.

The digest is deliberately actionable. It doesn't just list what's in the queue — it tells you what you can run right now. "3 jobs ready for the printer. 1 laser job waiting on a file. 2 plotter jobs need your photos." You read it at 9am, walk into the shop, and know exactly what to load up.

The 9:15 AM cron

QUEUE's heartbeat mirrors OWL's exactly — it's a cron job that fires at 9:15 AM, reads through the queue memory files, compiles all items that received research results overnight, groups them by machine, and generates the digest. Overnight research runs separately on items added the day before.

The timing is intentional: you wake up, check the morning brief from ATLAS at 7:30, get oriented to the day, then QUEUE fires at 9:15 with the shop-specific rundown — right as you're heading into the shop for the first run of the day.

Like OWL, the digest can also be triggered on-demand. Message QUEUE "what's ready?" and it compiles a quick status summary on the spot.

06 — Why I Built It

THE MACHINE
DESERVES BETTER

The Bambu Lab and the Thunder Laser are serious machines. They're fast, capable, and can produce real revenue. But they only produce revenue when they're running — and they only run when there's something in the queue.

Before QUEUE, "what to make next" lived in three places: a half-started Apple Note, a few screenshots in my camera roll, and my head. None of those places talk to each other, none of them tell me if a file exists, and none of them fire up on their own at 9am and say "hey, here's what you could run today."

The deeper issue is that every time I had to stop and figure out what to print next, it was a context switch — a decision I had to make under production pressure. QUEUE eliminates that decision entirely. The queue is always populated. The research is already done. When you walk into the shop, you already know what you're running.

QUEUE is also an ADHD accommodation — same as OWL. The machines don't care if you're focused or scattered. But the system has to work when you're not. QUEUE keeps the shop moving even when your brain is in three other places.

The research layer was the key insight. The moment QUEUE started automatically checking Thangs and MakerWorld before I even thought to look, it started saving real hours. Designs I would have modeled from scratch — already there, community-tested, printer-ready. That time goes back into the shop, not into CAD.

08 — Under the Hood

BUILT ON OWL'S
SKELETON

QUEUE runs as an OpenClaw agent on the M4 Pro Mac Mini, Telegram-bound, with a cron heartbeat that mirrors OWL's configuration exactly. The workspace structure is identical — SOUL.md, IDENTITY.md, AGENTS.md, HEARTBEAT.md — but the persona and instructions are fabrication-specific.

RUNTIME

OpenClaw agent runtime · Mac Mini M4 Pro · Node-based process · Session logs at ~/.openclaw/agents/queue/sessions/

CHANNEL

Telegram bot · Bound via openclaw agents bindings · Private bot, registered peer IDs only

HEARTBEAT

Cron fires at 9:15 AM daily · Reads queue memory files · Compiles research results · Delivers via Telegram + email digest

STORAGE

Queue entries stored as JSONL in ~/.openclaw/workspace-queue/queue/ · Research cache at research-cache/ · Status tracked per-item

The installer script creates the entire workspace in one command — directory structure, all MD persona files, cron registration, Telegram binding — so the agent is fully operational from a single terminal run. The pattern is identical to the Hauler and Sock Riot agent installers, all part of the same OpenClaw stack.

The research sweep runs asynchronously after each item is added — it doesn't block your Telegram response. You get the immediate capture confirmation first, then a follow-up message when research completes, usually within 30–60 seconds.
